% --- ABSTRACT ---
\begin{abstract}
We introduce \textbf{TrucoBench}, the first benchmark for evaluating large language models (LLMs) on Truco Paulista---a popular Latin American card game featuring imperfect information, bluffing, and a unique nested escalation mechanic. Unlike poker-based benchmarks, Truco's escalation system (truco $\to$ seis $\to$ nove $\to$ doze) creates a distinct strategic axis where players must reason about stake-raising, opponent modeling, and fold-or-fight decisions at multiple levels. We formalize Truco Paulista as a Partially Observable Stochastic Game (POSG) and evaluate frontier models using a 50-scenario diagnostic matrix and simulated round-robin tournaments. 

Our findings reveal a pervasive \textbf{Inverse Information Effect}: providing LLMs with explicit, verbose rule "cheat sheets" (LLMWiki) significantly degrades their strategic reasoning compared to minimal, standard prompting (dropping from 70.0\% to 65.6\% accuracy on Qwen 3.7 Max). Furthermore, we demonstrate that translating prompts to Portuguese---the game's cultural origin---imposes a severe reasoning penalty (dropping to 47.8\%), contradicting assumptions about cultural-linguistic alignment in logical reasoning tasks. Finally, we identify a persistent "Math Wall", where models fail almost entirely (5\% accuracy) at basic card sequencing and state tracking. TrucoBench is open-source at \url{https://github.com/ManzoliW/trucobench}.
\end{abstract}

% --- INTRODUCTION ---
\section{Introduction}

Large language models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks. A growing body of work evaluates LLMs on strategic games---particularly poker---as a testbed for reasoning under uncertainty and deception. However, the focus on poker leaves a gap: many culturally significant card games with distinct strategic properties remain unexplored as LLM benchmarks.

\textbf{Truco} is one of the most popular card games in Brazil and across Latin America. While it shares imperfect information with poker, it introduces a fundamentally different strategic axis: a \emph{nested escalation mechanic} where players can repeatedly raise the stakes of each round through a chain of calls (e.g., truco $\to$ seis $\to$ nove $\to$ doze). Each escalation forces the opponent into a three-way decision---accept, counter-raise, or fold---creating a rich space for bluffing, meta-reasoning, and risk assessment that is structurally distinct from poker's betting rounds. We support both the \textbf{Paulista} (variable manilhas) and \textbf{Mineiro} (fixed manilhas) variants, representing the two most common rule sets in Brazil.

We argue that Truco offers a complementary evaluation axis to existing game benchmarks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Escalation as nested bluffing.} Unlike poker's continuous bet sizing, Truco's discrete escalation chain creates clear decision points for commitment and credibility.
    \item \textbf{The Inverse Information Effect.} We demonstrate that increasing prompt density with explicit rule logic actively interferes with strategic inference.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Lingual Reasoning Penalties.} We show that evaluating models in the game's native language (Portuguese) unexpectedly penalizes logical accuracy.
\end{itemize}

% --- RELATED WORK ---
\section{Related Work}
Evaluating Large Language Models in game-theoretic environments, particularly those involving imperfect information, has become a key methodology for testing strategic reasoning capabilities. 

\textbf{Poker and Imperfect Information Benchmarks.} Poker is the traditional benchmark for imperfect information games. POKERBENCH \cite{xu2024pokerbench} systematically evaluates LLMs on poker scenarios, finding that vanilla LLMs struggle significantly compared to specialized solvers. To address this, frameworks like \textit{PokerSkill} \cite{pokerskill} demonstrate that LLMs require explicit "skill libraries" and structured interfaces to achieve expert-level play without heavy solver computation. TrucoBench complements this literature by introducing discrete escalation mechanics rather than continuous betting.

\textbf{LLM Game Agents and Automated Design.} Frameworks such as Cardiverse \cite{cardiverse} leverage LLMs for rapid card game prototyping, exploring how LLMs navigate game rule spaces and generate novel mechanics. While Cardiverse focuses on game generation, TrucoBench focuses strictly on isolating reasoning failures in an established cultural rule set, particularly testing Theory of Mind and deception.

As illustrated in Table~\ref{tab:diagnostics_v3}, the model demonstrates strong execution of psychological components and tie-breaking logic. However, several critical vulnerabilities and prompt sensitivities emerge.

\subsection{The Math Wall}
Across all conditions, the model struggled significantly with the "Math and Sequencing" category, hovering around ~51\% for Standard prompts and dropping to 35\% when using the verbose Wiki prompt. While the model can execute high-level bluffs, it struggles to maintain precise state-tracking of played cards and resolve the discrete ranking logic required to know if its hand mathematically guarantees a win.

\subsection{The Inverse Information Effect}
We initially theorized that providing the model with a dynamic "cheat sheet" of card rankings (the LLMWiki prompt) would alleviate the Math Wall. The opposite occurred for most models. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:multi_model}, transitioning from the bare-bones Standard prompt to the verbose LLMWiki prompt degraded overall performance significantly for Qwen (-15.0\%) and GPT-4o-mini (-14.0\%), and mildly for Gemini 2.5 Flash (-3.6\%). The additional context acted as a cognitive distraction, interfering with the models' established internal heuristics rather than augmenting them. Only Claude Haiku 4.5 showed immunity to this effect, remaining relatively stable (+0.6\%).

\subsection{The Language Penalty}
Because Truco is a Brazilian card game, we hypothesized that evaluating the model in Portuguese would align with the language of its pre-training data on the subject. However, we observed a strict split in model behavior. Prompting in Portuguese decreased the overall accuracy for Qwen (68.2\% $\to$ 67.1\%) and GPT-4o-mini (66.6\% $\to$ 62.9\%), constituting a "Language Penalty". Conversely, Claude Haiku 4.5 and Gemini 2.5 Flash both saw slight boosts in Portuguese (+2.4\% and +0.6\% respectively), suggesting their multilingual logical structures are more robust to domain translation.

\section{Conclusion}
TrucoBench provides a novel lens through which to evaluate LLM reasoning in imperfect information games. By utilizing a game with discrete escalation mechanics, we isolate psychological reasoning from traditional mathematical continuous betting. Our findings disprove common assumptions about prompt engineering: adding explicit "cheat sheet" rules degraded performance (The Inverse Information Effect), and prompting in the game's native cultural language severely penalized logical accuracy. Future work will expand to 4-player team dynamics and partner signaling.
